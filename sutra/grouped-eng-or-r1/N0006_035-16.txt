1. The First Chapter on the Cessation of Delight

155. First Section on the Cessation of Delight (1)

3. "Monks, when a monk sees the impermanent eye as impermanent, this is right view. Through correct contemplation, he becomes disenchanted. Through the cessation of delight comes the cessation of lust; through the cessation of lust comes the cessation of delight. When delight and lust cease, this is called the mind’s excellent liberation.

4-8. Monks, when a monk sees the impermanent ear... nose... tongue... body... mind as impermanent, this is right view. Through correct contemplation, he becomes disenchanted. Through the cessation of delight comes the cessation of lust; through the cessation of lust comes the cessation of delight. When delight and lust cease, this is called the mind’s excellent liberation."

156. Second Section on the Cessation of Delight (2)

3. "Monks, when a monk sees impermanent forms as impermanent, this is right view. Through correct contemplation, he becomes disenchanted. Through the cessation of delight comes the cessation of lust; through the cessation of lust comes the cessation of delight. When delight and lust cease, this is called the mind’s excellent liberation.

4-8. Monks, when a monk sees impermanent sounds... smells... tastes... touches... mental phenomena as impermanent, this is right view. Through correct contemplation, he becomes disenchanted. Through the cessation of delight comes the cessation of lust; through the cessation of lust comes the cessation of delight. When delight and lust cease, this is called the mind’s excellent liberation."

157. Third Section on the Cessation of Delight (3)

3. "Monks, correctly reflect on the eye and truly recognize the eye’s impermanence. A monk who correctly reflects on the eye and truly recognizes its impermanence becomes disenchanted with the eye. Through the cessation of delight comes the cessation of lust; through the cessation of lust comes the cessation of delight. When delight and lust cease, this is called the mind’s excellent liberation.

4-8. Monks, correctly reflect on the ear... nose... tongue... body... mind and truly recognize the mind’s impermanence. A monk who correctly reflects on the mind and truly recognizes its impermanence becomes disenchanted with the mind. Through the cessation of delight comes the cessation of lust; through the cessation of lust comes the cessation of delight. When delight and lust cease, this is called the mind’s excellent liberation."

158. Fourth Section on the Cessation of Delight (4)

3. "Monks, correctly reflect on forms and truly recognize their impermanence. A monk who correctly reflects on forms and truly recognizes their impermanence becomes disenchanted with forms. Through the cessation of delight comes the cessation of lust; through the cessation of lust comes the cessation of delight. When delight and lust cease, this is called the mind’s excellent liberation.

4-8. Monks, correctly reflect on sounds... smells... tastes... touches... mental phenomena and truly recognize their impermanence. A monk who correctly reflects on mental phenomena and truly recognizes their impermanence becomes disenchanted with mental phenomena. Through the cessation of delight comes the cessation of lust; through the cessation of lust comes the cessation of delight. When delight and lust cease, this is called the mind’s excellent liberation."

159. Fifth Section: Jīvaka’s Mango Grove (1)

1. At that time, the Blessed One was dwelling at Jīvaka’s mango grove near Rājagaha.

2. There, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Monks..."

3. "Monks, cultivate concentration. A monk who attains concentration understands things as they really are. What does he understand as they really are?

4. 'The eye is impermanent' is understood as it really is. 'Forms are impermanent' is understood as they really are. 'Eye-consciousness is impermanent' is understood as it really is. 'Eye-contact is impermanent' is understood as it really is. 'Whatever feeling arises dependent on eye-contact—whether pleasant, painful, or neither painful nor pleasant—is impermanent' is understood as it really is.

5;9. 'The ear... nose... tongue... body... mind is impermanent' is understood as it really is. 'Mental phenomena are impermanent' is understood as they really are. 'Mind-consciousness is impermanent' is understood as it really is. 'Mind-contact is impermanent' is understood as it really is. 'Whatever feeling arises dependent on mind-contact—whether pleasant, painful, or neither painful nor pleasant—is impermanent' is understood as it really is.

10. Monks, cultivate concentration. A monk who attains concentration understands things as they really are."

160. Sixth Section: Jīvaka’s Mango Grove (2)

1. At that time, the Blessed One was dwelling at Jīvaka’s mango grove near Rājagaha.

2. There, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Monks..."

3. "Monks, focus your minds on serene contemplation. A monk who enters serene contemplation understands things as they really are. What does he understand as they really are?

4;9. 'The eye is impermanent' is understood as it really is... 'The mind is impermanent' is understood as it really is... 'Whatever feeling arises dependent on mind-contact—whether pleasant, painful, or neither painful nor pleasant—is impermanent' is understood as it really is.

10. Monks, focus your minds on serene contemplation. A monk who enters serene contemplation understands things as they really are."

161. Seventh Section: Koṭṭhita (1)

2. At that time, the Venerable Mahā Koṭṭhita approached the Blessed One...

3. Sitting to one side, he said: "Venerable Sir, may the Blessed One teach me the Dhamma in brief. Having heard it, I will dwell alone, secluded, diligent, ardent, and resolute."

"Koṭṭhita, you should abandon desire for what is impermanent. What is impermanent?

4. Koṭṭhita, the eye is impermanent: abandon desire for it. Forms are impermanent: abandon desire for them. Eye-consciousness is impermanent: abandon desire for it. Eye-contact is impermanent: abandon desire for it. Whatever feeling arises dependent on eye-contact—whether pleasant, painful, or neither painful nor pleasant—is impermanent: abandon desire for it.

5;9. Koṭṭhita, the ear... nose... tongue... body... mind is impermanent: abandon desire for it. Mental phenomena are impermanent: abandon desire for them. Mind-consciousness is impermanent: abandon desire for it. Mind-contact is impermanent: abandon desire for it. Whatever feeling arises dependent on mind-contact—whether pleasant, painful, or neither painful nor pleasant—is impermanent: abandon desire for it.

10. Koṭṭhita, you should abandon desire for what is impermanent."

162. Eighth Section: Koṭṭhita (2)

2-3. At that time, the Venerable Koṭṭhita... "diligent, ardent, and resolute."

"Koṭṭhita, you should abandon desire for what is suffering. What is suffering?

4. Koṭṭhita, the eye is suffering: abandon desire for it. Forms are suffering: abandon desire for them. Eye-consciousness is suffering: abandon desire for it. Eye-contact is suffering: abandon desire for it. Whatever feeling arises dependent on eye-contact—whether pleasant, painful, or neither painful nor pleasant—is suffering: abandon desire for it.

5;9. Koṭṭhita, the ear... nose... tongue... body... mind is suffering... Whatever feeling arises dependent on mind-contact—whether pleasant, painful, or neither painful nor pleasant—is suffering: abandon desire for it.

10. Koṭṭhita, you should abandon desire for what is suffering."

163. Ninth Section: Koṭṭhita (3)

2-3. At that time, the Venerable Koṭṭhita... "diligent, ardent, and resolute."

"Koṭṭhita, you should abandon desire for what is non-self. What is non-self?

4. Koṭṭhita, the eye is non-self: abandon desire for it. Forms are non-self: abandon desire for them. Eye-consciousness is non-self: abandon desire for it. Eye-contact is non-self: abandon desire for it. Whatever feeling arises dependent on eye-contact—whether pleasant, painful, or neither painful nor pleasant—is non-self: abandon desire for it.

5;9. Koṭṭhita, the ear... nose... tongue... body... mind is non-self... Whatever feeling arises dependent on mind-contact—whether pleasant, painful, or neither painful nor pleasant—is non-self: abandon desire for it.

10. Koṭṭhita, you should abandon desire for what is non-self."

164. Tenth Section: Wrong View

2. Then a monk approached the Blessed One...

3. Sitting to one side, he asked: "Venerable Sir, how should one know and see to abandon wrong view?"

4;9. "Monk, knowing and seeing the eye as impermanent, one abandons wrong view. Knowing and seeing forms... eye-consciousness... eye-contact... feelings arising from eye-contact as impermanent, one abandons wrong view... Knowing and seeing the mind... mental phenomena... mind-consciousness... mind-contact... feelings arising from mind-contact as impermanent, one abandons wrong view.

10. Monk, one who knows and sees thus abandons wrong view."

165. Eleventh Section: Koṭṭhita identity View

2;3. ...asked: "Venerable Sir, how should one know and see to abandon identity view?"

4;9. "Monk, knowing and seeing the eye as suffering, one abandons identity view. Knowing and seeing forms... eye-consciousness... eye-contact... feelings arising from eye-contact as suffering, one abandons identity view... Knowing and seeing the mind... mental phenomena... mind-contact... feelings arising from mind-contact as suffering, one abandons identity view.

10. Monk, one who knows and sees thus abandons identity view."

166. Twelfth Section: Self

2;3. ...asked: "Venerable Sir, how should one know and see to abandon the view of self?"

4;9. "Monk, knowing and seeing the eye as non-self, one abandons the view of self. Knowing and seeing forms... eye-consciousness... eye-contact... feelings arising from eye-contact as non-self, one abandons the view of self... Knowing and seeing the mind... mental phenomena... mind-contact... feelings arising from mind-contact as non-self, one abandons the view of self.

10. Monk, one who knows and sees thus abandons the view of self."

End of the First Chapter on the Cessation of Delight

Summary Verses:  
Four on the cessation of delight,  
Two on Jīvaka’s mango grove,  
Three on Koṭṭhita,  
Then wrong view, identity view,  
And the view of self.
