5. New and Old  

145. 1. Kamma  

2. "Monks, I will teach you new and old kamma, the cessation of kamma, and the path leading to the cessation of kamma. Listen well and attend closely. I will speak."  

3. "Monks, what is old kamma? The eye is formed by old kamma, produced by volitional activities, accumulated through acts, thoughts, and feelings. The ear... nose... tongue... body... mind is formed by old kamma, produced by volitional activities, accumulated through acts, thoughts, and feelings. Monks, this is called old kamma."  

4. "Monks, what is new kamma? Any kamma performed now through body, speech, or mind. Monks, this is called new kamma."  

5. "Monks, what is the cessation of kamma? The liberation attained through the cessation of bodily, verbal, and mental kamma. Monks, this is called the cessation of kamma."  

6. "Monks, what is the path leading to the cessation of kamma? It is the Noble Eightfold Path: right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration. Monks, this is called the path leading to the cessation of kamma."  

7. "Thus, monks, I have taught you old kamma, new kamma, the cessation of kamma, and the path leading to the cessation of kamma."  

8. "Monks, whatever a teacher out of compassion should do for disciples seeking their welfare, I have done for you."  

9. "Monks, meditate at the foot of trees or in empty huts. Do not be heedless, so you will not regret it later. This is my instruction."  

146. 2. Effective (1)  

2. "Monks, I will teach you the path effective for attaining Nibbāna. Listen well... What is the path effective for attaining Nibbāna?"  

3. "Here, a monk sees the eye as impermanent, sees forms as impermanent, sees eye-consciousness as impermanent, sees eye-contact as impermanent. Whatever feeling arises from eye-contact—pleasant, painful, or neutral—he sees that too as impermanent."  

4-8. "He sees the ear... nose... tongue... body... mind as impermanent, sees mental phenomena as impermanent, sees mind-consciousness as impermanent, sees mind-contact as impermanent. Whatever feeling arises from mind-contact—pleasant, painful, or neutral—he sees that too as impermanent."  

9. "Monks, this is the path effective for attaining Nibbāna."  

147. 3. Effective (2)  

2. "Monks, I will teach you the path effective for attaining Nibbāna... What is the path effective for attaining Nibbāna?"  

3. "Here, a monk sees the eye as suffering, sees forms as suffering, sees eye-consciousness as suffering, sees eye-contact as suffering. Whatever feeling arises from eye-contact—pleasant, painful, or neutral—he sees that too as suffering."  

4-8. "...ear... nose... tongue... body... mind..."  

9. "Monks, this is the path effective for attaining Nibbāna."  

148. 4. Effective (3)  

2. "Monks, I will teach you the path effective for attaining Nibbāna... What is the path effective for attaining Nibbāna?"  

3. "Here, a monk sees the eye as non-self, sees forms as non-self, sees eye-consciousness as non-self, sees eye-contact as non-self. Whatever feeling arises from eye-contact—pleasant, painful, or neutral—he sees that too as non-self."  

4-8. "...ear... nose... tongue... body... mind..."  

9. "Monks, this is the path effective for attaining Nibbāna."  

149. 5. Effective (4)  

2. "Monks, I will teach you the path effective for attaining Nibbāna... What is the path effective for attaining Nibbāna?"  

3. "Monks, what do you think: Is the eye permanent or impermanent?" —"Impermanent, venerable sir." —"Is what is impermanent suffering or happiness?" —"Suffering, venerable sir." —"Is it fitting to regard what is impermanent, suffering, and subject to change as 'This is mine, this I am, this is my self'?" —"No, venerable sir."  

4-8. (Similarly for ear... mind and related phenomena.)  

9. "Seeing thus, a learned noble disciple becomes disenchanted with the eye... mind. Through disenchantment, he becomes dispassionate... He understands: 'Birth is destroyed, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is no more coming to any state of being.'"  

10. "Monks, this is the path effective for attaining Nibbāna."  

150. 6. Internal Abiding  

3. "Monks, dwell in a holy life without internal abiding or guidance. A monk dwelling with internal abiding and guidance dwells in suffering, not in comfort. One dwelling without internal abiding or guidance dwells comfortably."  

4-12. Explains how desire and ill-will arising through the senses constitute "internal abiding" and "guidance," leading to suffering. Absence of such defilements constitutes freedom.  

13-20. (Detailed contrast between defiled and undefiled states.)  

151. 7. What Merit?  

3. "If wanderers of other sects ask: 'What is the purpose of living the holy life under the ascetic Gotama?' answer: 'Friends, it is for the full understanding of suffering.'"  

4-12. Explains each sense base and related phenomena as the suffering to be fully understood.  

152. 8. With Cause  

3. "Monks, a monk with valid cause—not by faith, preference, oral tradition, reasoned thought, or acceptance of a view—directly knows: 'Birth is destroyed...'"  

4-13. Describes how one knows the presence or absence of greed, hatred, and delusion through wisdom, establishing the validity of liberation.  

153. 9. Faculties  

3-4. A monk asks about "complete faculties." The Buddha explains: one who sees the arising and vanishing of each sense faculty, becomes disenchanted, liberated through non-clinging.  

154. 10. Dhamma Teacher  

3-4. A monk asks about "one who teaches Dhamma." The Buddha replies: one teaching for disenchantment, dispassion, and cessation regarding the senses is a true Dhamma teacher.  

End of the New and Old Chapter.
