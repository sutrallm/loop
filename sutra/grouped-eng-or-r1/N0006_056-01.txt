1. First: Concentration  

1.1. Savatthi.  

1.2. "Bhikkhus, cultivate concentration! A bhikkhu who attains concentration understands things as they truly are. What does he understand as they truly are?  

1.3. He understands as it truly is: 'This is suffering.' He understands as it truly is: 'This is the origin of suffering.' He understands as it truly is: 'This is the cessation of suffering.' He understands as it truly is: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'  

1.4. Bhikkhus, cultivate concentration! A bhikkhu who attains concentration understands things as they truly are. Therefore, bhikkhus, strive with the thought: 'This is suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the origin of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'"  

2. Second: Seclusion  

2.1. "Bhikkhus, devote yourselves to seclusion! A bhikkhu who dwells in seclusion understands things as they truly are. What does he understand as they truly are?  

2.2. He understands as it truly is: 'This is suffering.' He understands as it truly is: 'This is the origin of suffering.' He understands as it truly is: 'This is the cessation of suffering.' He understands as it truly is: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'  

2.3. Bhikkhus, devote yourselves to seclusion! A bhikkhu who dwells in seclusion understands things as they truly are. Therefore, bhikkhus, strive with the thought: 'This is suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the origin of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'"  

3. Third: The Virtuous Man (1)  

3.1. "Bhikkhus, in the past, all virtuous men who left the household life to become ascetics did so to realize these Four Noble Truths as they truly are.  

3.2. Bhikkhus, in the future, all virtuous men who will leave the household life to become ascetics will do so to realize these Four Noble Truths as they truly are.  

3.3. Bhikkhus, in the present, all virtuous men who have left the household life to become ascetics do so to realize these Four Noble Truths as they truly are. What are the Four Noble Truths?  

3.4. They are: the noble truth of suffering, the noble truth of the origin of suffering, the noble truth of the cessation of suffering, and the noble truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering.  

3.5. Therefore, bhikkhus, strive with the thought: 'This is suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the origin of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'"  

4. Fourth: The Virtuous Man (2)  

4.1. "Bhikkhus, in the past, virtuous men who left home and realized things as they truly are did so by realizing these Four Noble Truths as they truly are.  

4.2. Bhikkhus, in the future, virtuous men who will leave home and realize things as they truly are will do so by realizing these Four Noble Truths as they truly are.  

4.3. Bhikkhus, in the present, virtuous men who leave home and realize things as they truly are do so by realizing these Four Noble Truths as they truly are. What are the Four Noble Truths?  

4.4. They are: the noble truth of suffering, the noble truth of the origin of suffering, the noble truth of the cessation of suffering, and the noble truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering.  

4.5. Therefore, bhikkhus, strive with the thought: 'This is suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the origin of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'"  

5. Fifth: Ascetics and Brahmins (1)  

5.1. "Bhikkhus, in the past, ascetics and brahmins who fully awakened to things as they truly are did so by awakening to these Four Noble Truths.  

5.2. Bhikkhus, in the future, ascetics and brahmins who will fully awaken to things as they truly are will do so by awakening to these Four Noble Truths.  

5.3. Bhikkhus, in the present, ascetics and brahmins who fully awaken to things as they truly are do so by awakening to these Four Noble Truths. What are the Four Noble Truths?  

5.4. They are: the noble truth of suffering, the noble truth of the origin of suffering, the noble truth of the cessation of suffering, and the noble truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering.  

5.5. Therefore, bhikkhus, strive with the thought: 'This is suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the origin of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'"  

6. Sixth: Ascetics and Brahmins (2)  

6.1. "Bhikkhus, in the past, the teachings proclaimed by ascetics and brahmins who fully awakened to things as they truly are were proclaimed through awakening to these Four Noble Truths.  

6.2. Bhikkhus, in the future, the teachings proclaimed by ascetics and brahmins who will fully awaken to things as they truly are will be proclaimed through awakening to these Four Noble Truths.  

6.3. Bhikkhus, in the present, the teachings proclaimed by ascetics and brahmins who fully awaken to things as they truly are are proclaimed through awakening to these Four Noble Truths. What are the Four Noble Truths?  

6.4. They are: the noble truth of suffering, the noble truth of the origin of suffering, the noble truth of the cessation of suffering, and the noble truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering.  

6.5. Therefore, bhikkhus, strive with the thought: 'This is suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the origin of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'"  

7. Seventh: Thoughts  

7.1. "Bhikkhus, do not pursue evil unwholesome thoughts—thoughts of sensuality, malice, or harm. Why?  

7.2. These thoughts do not lead to welfare, nor to the fundamentals of the spiritual life, nor to dispassion, cessation, peace, direct knowledge, enlightenment, or Nibbāna.  

7.3. When you think, bhikkhus, think: 'This is suffering,' think: 'This is the origin of suffering,' think: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' think: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.' Why?  

7.4. These thoughts lead to welfare, to the fundamentals of the spiritual life, and to dispassion, cessation, peace, direct knowledge, enlightenment, and Nibbāna. Therefore, bhikkhus, strive with the thought: 'This is suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the origin of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'"  

8. Eighth: Speculations  

8.1. "Bhikkhus, do not engage in unwholesome speculative views, such as: 'The world is eternal,' 'The world is not eternal,' 'The world is finite,' 'The world is infinite,' 'The soul and the body are the same,' 'The soul and the body are different,' 'After death, a Tathāgata exists,' 'After death, a Tathāgata does not exist,' 'After death, a Tathāgata both exists and does not exist,' 'After death, a Tathāgata neither exists nor does not exist.' Why?  

8.2. These speculations do not lead to welfare, nor to the fundamentals of the spiritual life, nor to dispassion, cessation, peace, direct knowledge, enlightenment, or Nibbāna.  

8.3. When you speculate, bhikkhus, speculate: 'This is suffering,' speculate: 'This is the origin of suffering,' speculate: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' speculate: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.' Why?  

8.4. These speculations lead to welfare, to the fundamentals of the spiritual life, and to dispassion, cessation, peace, direct knowledge, enlightenment, and Nibbāna. Therefore, bhikkhus, strive with the thought: 'This is suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the origin of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'"  

9. Ninth: Disputes  

9.1. "Bhikkhus, do not engage in contentious disputes, such as: 'You don’t understand this Teaching and Discipline—I do! How could you understand it? You’re wrong—I’m right! I stay on topic—you don’t! You said later what should be said first and first what should be said later! Your claim is groundless! You’re obsessed with your own views! Admit defeat—you’re refuted!' Why?  

9.2. These disputes do not lead to welfare, nor to the fundamentals of the spiritual life, nor to dispassion, cessation, peace, direct knowledge, enlightenment, or Nibbāna.  

9.3. When you discuss, bhikkhus, discuss: 'This is suffering,' discuss: 'This is the origin of suffering,' discuss: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' discuss: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.' Why?  

9.4. These discussions lead to welfare, to the fundamentals of the spiritual life, and to dispassion, cessation, peace, direct knowledge, enlightenment, and Nibbāna. Therefore, bhikkhus, strive with the thought: 'This is suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the origin of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'"  

10. Tenth: Topics  

10.1. "Bhikkhus, do not engage in frivolous talk, such as talk of kings, thieves, ministers, armies, dangers, battles, food, drink, clothing, beds, garlands, perfumes, relatives, vehicles, villages, towns, cities, regions, women, heroes, gossip, wells, ghosts, speculations, worldly matters, or the origin of the ocean. Why?  

10.2. These topics do not lead to welfare, nor to the fundamentals of the spiritual life, nor to dispassion, cessation, peace, direct knowledge, enlightenment, or Nibbāna.  

10.3. When you converse, bhikkhus, converse: 'This is suffering,' converse: 'This is the origin of suffering,' converse: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' converse: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.' Why?  

10.4. These topics lead to welfare, to the fundamentals of the spiritual life, and to dispassion, cessation, peace, direct knowledge, enlightenment, and Nibbāna. Therefore, bhikkhus, strive with the thought: 'This is suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the origin of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the cessation of suffering,' strive with the thought: 'This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.'"  

End of the First Chapter: Concentration
